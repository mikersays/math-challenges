Approaches to P vs NP

Decades of work have produced a rich toolkit for separating complexity classes — and an equally rich understanding of why each tool stalls. Below is a barrier-aware map of the major research programs the swarm explored, each with an honest summary and its key theorems.

How to read this map

Any valid resolution of \(\mathsf{P}\) vs \(\mathsf{NP}\) must simultaneously be non-relativizing, non-natural, and non-algebrizing. Three of the sections below describe those barriers themselves; the rest describe techniques and the barriers they run into. No known method clears all three hurdles at once.

Barrier & technique

Diagonalization & relativization

Diagonalization — the Cantor/Turing technique of building a machine that disagrees with every machine in an enumeration — powers the time and space hierarchy theorems and the undecidability of the halting problem. But these proofs relativize: they hold unchanged when every machine is given an oracle. Baker, Gill, and Solovay showed there exist oracles making \(\mathsf{P}^{A}=\mathsf{NP}^{A}\) and others making \(\mathsf{P}^{B}\neq\mathsf{NP}^{B}\), so no relativizing argument can settle the question either way.

Key results

Time Hierarchy Theorem (Hartmanis–Stearns, 1965): for time-constructible \(f,g\) with \(f \log f = o(g)\), \(\mathsf{DTIME}(f) \subsetneq \mathsf{DTIME}(g)\).

Baker–Gill–Solovay (1975): there exist oracles \(A,B\) with \(\mathsf{P}^{A}=\mathsf{NP}^{A}\) and \(\mathsf{P}^{B}\neq\mathsf{NP}^{B}\). Hence \(\mathsf{P}\) vs \(\mathsf{NP}\) cannot be resolved by any relativizing proof.

Technique

Boolean circuit complexity

Rather than bound running time, one bounds the number of gates needed to compute a function on \(n\)-bit inputs. Proving any explicit \(\mathsf{NP}\) function requires super-polynomial circuits would give \(\mathsf{NP}\not\subseteq\mathsf{P/poly}\), and hence \(\mathsf{P}\neq\mathsf{NP}\). Strong unconditional lower bounds exist for restricted classes (monotone, bounded-depth), but for general circuits the best known bound for an explicit function is only about \(5n\) gates.

Key results

Razborov (1985): monotone circuits for the \(k\)-clique problem require size \(n^{\Omega(k)}\), super-polynomial for suitable \(k\).

Håstad’s switching lemma (1986): \(\mathsf{PARITY}\) requires depth-\(d\) \(\mathsf{AC}^0\) circuits of size \(2^{\Omega(n^{1/(d-1)})}\), so \(\mathsf{PARITY}\notin\mathsf{AC}^0\).

Barrier

Natural proofs barrier

Razborov and Rudich observed that essentially all known circuit lower bounds share a structure they called natural: a property of truth tables that is constructive (efficiently checkable), large (held by a random function), and useful (fails for easy functions). They proved that a natural property usable against \(\mathsf{P/poly}\) would yield a sub-exponential algorithm breaking strong pseudorandom generators — objects widely believed to exist. So the very combinatorial style that works on restricted classes cannot, by itself, reach \(\mathsf{P/poly}\).

Key result

Razborov–Rudich (1994/97): if strong one-way functions (equivalently, exponentially hard pseudorandom generators) exist, then there is no natural property useful against \(\mathsf{P/poly}\). A successful proof must therefore be non-natural — it must sacrifice constructivity or largeness.

Technique

Proof complexity

Proof complexity asks how long the shortest proof of a tautology must be in a given formal system. By the Cook–Reckhow theorem, \(\mathsf{NP}=\mathsf{coNP}\) iff some propositional proof system is polynomially bounded; ruling out short proofs in ever-stronger systems is a program aimed at that collapse. Strong lower bounds are known for weak systems (resolution, bounded-depth Frege), but no super-polynomial lower bound is known for Frege or Extended Frege — a notably barrier-resistant frontier.

Key results

Cook–Reckhow (1979): a polynomially-bounded proof system exists iff \(\mathsf{NP}=\mathsf{coNP}\).

Haken (1985): resolution refutations of the pigeonhole principle \(\mathrm{PHP}^{n+1}_{n}\) require size \(2^{\Omega(n)}\).

Technique

Geometric Complexity Theory (GCT)

Proposed by Mulmuley and Sohoni, GCT recasts lower bounds as questions in algebraic geometry and representation theory: separate the orbit closures of the permanent and determinant by exhibiting a representation-theoretic obstruction — a symmetry pattern present on one side but not the other. The program is appealing because it aims to be inherently non-natural, but the original occurrence-obstruction form was shown impossible, redirecting effort toward multiplicity obstructions and positive plethysm / Kronecker-coefficient results.

Key results

Mulmuley–Sohoni (2001/2008): formulated \(\overline{\mathrm{VP}}\neq\overline{\mathrm{VNP}}\) (permanent vs determinant) as separation of group-orbit closures via representation-theoretic obstructions.

Bürgisser–Ikenmeyer–Panova (2016/2019): occurrence obstructions cannot prove the permanent-versus-determinant lower bound, ruling out the program’s original strategy.

Technique

Algebraic complexity: VP vs VNP

Valiant’s algebraic analogue replaces Boolean circuits with arithmetic circuits over a field, and asks whether the permanent (the canonical \(\mathrm{VNP}\)-complete polynomial) needs super-polynomial circuits. \(\mathrm{VP}\neq\mathrm{VNP}\) is widely seen as a structured stepping stone toward \(\mathsf{P}\neq\mathsf{NP}\). Progress ran into the depth-four chasm: lower bounds for general circuits reduce to depth-4 circuits, but the strongest measure used there — shifted partial derivatives — is provably capped just below what is needed.

Key results

Valiant (1979): the permanent is \(\mathrm{VNP}\)-complete; the determinant lies in \(\mathrm{VP}\).

Agrawal–Vinay; Tavenas; Gupta–Kamath–Kayal–Saptharishi (2008–2014): general arithmetic lower bounds reduce to homogeneous depth-4 circuits (the “chasm at depth four”), where near-tight \(n^{\Omega(\sqrt{n})}\) bounds are known but fall short of a separation.

Barrier

Algebrization barrier

Arithmetization — extending Boolean functions to low-degree polynomials — drove the interactive-proof breakthroughs (\(\mathsf{IP}=\mathsf{PSPACE}\)) and is genuinely non-relativizing. Aaronson and Wigderson distilled its reach into algebrization: arguments where one machine may query a low-degree algebraic extension of the other’s oracle. They showed that \(\mathsf{P}\) vs \(\mathsf{NP}\) cannot be settled by algebrizing techniques, extending the relativization barrier to cover the arithmetization toolkit too.

Key result

Aaronson–Wigderson (2008): there are algebraic oracle extensions relative to which \(\mathsf{P}=\mathsf{NP}\) and others relative to which \(\mathsf{P}\neq\mathsf{NP}\). Any resolution must be non-algebrizing; it is not even known whether algebrization is the final barrier.

Technique

Algorithms & upper bounds

The flip side of separation is the search for fast algorithms — and, paradoxically, fast algorithms can prove lower bounds. Williams’ algorithmic method turns a faster-than-brute-force Circuit-SAT algorithm into a circuit lower bound; it is provably non-natural and non-relativizing, making it one of the most promising routes (the open challenge is scaling it from \(\mathsf{NEXP}\)/\(\mathsf{ACC}^0\) down to \(\mathsf{NP}\) vs \(\mathsf{P/poly}\)). For SAT itself, every known algorithm remains “width-local” and degrades toward brute force, consistent with the Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis.

Key results

Williams (2011/2014): \(\mathsf{NEXP}\not\subseteq\mathsf{ACC}^0\), proved by converting a slightly-faster \(\mathsf{ACC}^0\)-SAT algorithm into a lower bound — the algorithmic method.

Impagliazzo–Paturi (SETH, 2001): conjecturally, for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is a \(k\) such that \(k\)-SAT cannot be solved in \(O(2^{(1-\varepsilon)n})\) time — brute force is essentially optimal.

Current status

P vs NP remains open

The swarm did not resolve \(\mathsf{P}\) vs \(\mathsf{NP}\), and produced no new unconditional separation or collapse. Its value is a precise, barrier-aware map of why every mature technique stalls. The three classical barriers — relativization (Baker–Gill–Solovay 1975), natural proofs (Razborov–Rudich 1994), and algebrization (Aaronson–Wigderson 2008) — together rule out the entire classical toolkit. Any valid proof must be simultaneously non-relativizing, non-natural, and non-algebrizing, and no known technique satisfies all three.

Concretely: we have no super-polynomial lower bound for any explicit \(\mathsf{NP}\) problem on a general basis (best \(\approx 5n\) gates), none for Frege, none for general arithmetic circuits (stuck near \(n\log n\)); the occurrence-obstruction form of GCT is provably impossible; and every known SAT algorithm degrades to brute force consistent with SETH. \(\mathsf{P}=\mathsf{NP}\) is not refuted — only judged unlikely on the weight of evidence, since the barriers are symmetric.

The most plausible paths forward: scaling Williams’ algorithmic method below \(\mathsf{NEXP}\); a meta-complexity / hardness-magnification result that survives a naturalness re-check; or new positive representation-theory results for the GCT and algebraic routes. The mathematics needed to cross this frontier does not yet exist.
