A documented brute-force effort

We tried hard to solve it. Here is exactly how we failed.

We pointed swarms of expert AI agents at P vs NP and asked them to actually attempt a proof — not to summarize the field, but to push for a solution. Across two rounds they produced nine genuine proof attempts. Every one of them was independently attacked by skeptics and ruled broken. This page documents that effort honestly, first in plain language, then in detail.

The honest headline first

We did not solve P vs NP. Nobody has. It remains an open Millennium Prize problem. What follows is not a proof and contains no proof. Its value is the opposite of a triumphant announcement: a precise, externally checked record of why every modern line of attack stops where it does. A well-documented dead end is the real product here.

In plain language

What we did, without the jargon

Imagine a vault that has resisted the world's best safecrackers for fifty years. We did not bring a smarter safecracker. We brought a whole crew, told each member to try a different tool, and hired a second crew whose only job was to prove the first crew wrong.

1 Many tries at once

Instead of one attempt, we ran many in parallel — each an expert agent taking a different angle of attack on the problem at the same time.

2 Built-in skeptics

Every attempt was handed to a panel of critics whose job was to break it: checking the logic, the numbers, the references, and whether it secretly assumed what it was trying to prove.

3 An honest referee

A neutral judge weighed each attempt against its critics and recorded a verdict. No attempt was allowed to grade its own homework.

The result, in one breath:

All nine attempts failed — and, strikingly, they nearly all failed for the same underlying reason. There is a kind of mathematical "wall" that the known techniques keep running into. Each clever attempt was really just a new disguise for hitting that same wall. When we took our single best attempt and tried to patch the exact spot where it broke, we discovered the patch is impossible — fixing it would require disproving a fact that mathematicians have already proven is true.

Why couldn't the AI just brute-force it?

Because the obstacle is not a lack of effort, speed, or cleverness — it is a theorem. Mathematicians have proven that whole families of proof techniques cannot settle this question. A faster or more tireless attempt runs into the same proven barrier; it just gets there sooner.

So was it a waste?

No. We now have a sharper map of the dead ends than we started with: nine attacks, each traced to the precise step where it dies, all checked by independent critics. That is how real progress on hard problems is made — by ruling things out cleanly and knowing exactly why.

Could anyone ever solve it?

Maybe. It would take a genuinely new idea that slips past all the known barriers at once — something no one has yet found. Our effort didn't find it, but it did help pin down what such an idea would need to do.

How the swarm worked

Attempt, attack, adjudicate

Each round followed the same adversarial pipeline. The point of the design is that nothing survives unless it survives hostile, independent review.

A

Attempt

Independent expert-persona agents each push one strategy as far as it honestly goes, labeling every step as either an established result or an unverified novel claim, and flagging their own weakest link.

B

Audit & red-team

A dedicated barrier auditor checks the three classical obstructions, and a multi-lens red-team attacks rigor, the literature, the actual numbers, and hidden circularity — defaulting to "broken" unless a step is airtight.

C

Adjudicate & repair

A neutral referee rules on each attempt and scores how broken it is. In round two, the least-broken attempt got a dedicated repair team — whose fix was then itself re-attacked.

Technical · Round one

Four attacks on the most promising frontiers

The first round took the four research directions generally considered most likely to eventually crack the problem. Each was attacked by a three-lens red-team. All four were ruled broken, 3/3.

Algorithmic method Broken · 3/3

Scaling Williams' method below NEXP

Build a recursive "tower" of the faster-algorithm-implies-lower-bound technique to push it from \(\mathrm{NEXP}\) down toward \(\mathrm{NP}\).

Where it died

Circularity. The per-rung "glue" cost compounds to \(2^{\Theta(N^2)}\) instead of polynomial, and the only repair is provably equivalent to the separation it was trying to prove — conservation of difficulty. The easy-witness lever is also information-vacuous at the \(\mathrm{NP}\) scale.

Meta-complexity Broken · 3/3

Hardness magnification via MCSP

Prove a tiny lower bound for a meta-complexity problem and let known magnification theorems amplify it into a breakthrough.

Where it died

The target quantity is \(O(\log N)\)-Lipschitz, so its Fourier mass sits at low levels — the opposite of what the argument needs — and any patched route is blocked by the locality barrier exactly at the magnifiable parameter. The amplifier was sound; the weak bound it needed is forbidden.

Geometric Complexity Theory Broken · 3/3

Multiplicity obstructions for permanent vs determinant

Use representation-theoretic multiplicities (after occurrence obstructions were proven dead) to separate the permanent from the determinant.

Where it died

The hard half — a lower bound on the permanent-side multiplicity — was merely asserted. The "lockstep" problem means the construction that shrinks one side shrinks the other too, pointing against the very inequality it needed.

Proof complexity Broken · 3/3

Proof-complexity generators toward Frege

Construct a hard tautology family forcing a super-polynomial Frege lower bound, which would give \(\mathrm{NP}\ne\mathrm{coNP}\).

Where it died

A vacuous distinguishing step (the relevant set has vanishing density, so a trivial circuit separates it for free) and a misapplied witnessing theorem. The argument also re-imports an unproven strong circuit lower bound — the very wall it hoped to bypass.

Round-one verdict: no attempt survived. The one direction the referees flagged as genuinely live and not-yet-circular was scaling the algorithmic method on \(\mathrm{ACC}^0\circ\mathrm{THR}\) — which became the entire target of round two.

Technical · Round two

Maximum-rigor assault on the one live lead

We threw everything at the live lead: five independent attempts, each with a dedicated barrier audit, a five-lens red-team (barrier, rigor, literature, quantitative, circularity), neutral adjudication, and a repair round on the least-broken attempt. Roughly 42 agents and 2.1M tokens.

The unifying finding

All five attempts — and the parent attempt from round one — die on a single object: the unconditional \(\Theta(\sqrt{n})\) approximate-degree lower bound for threshold functions (Paturi; Nisan–Szegedy; Sherstov). Each route just meets a different face of it: polynomial degree, sparsity, matrix-multiplication dimension, sign-rank, or core-shell measure. The method conserves the wall rather than defeating it.

AttemptRed-teamBrokennessWhere it died
Probabilistic polynomials + correlation + matmul 5/5 broken 7/10 least broken Dichotomy lemma vacuous at constant gap; repair proven impossible.
GAP-SAT / #SAT derandomization 5/5 broken 7/10 Cross-block threshold join hits the \(\Theta(\sqrt n)\) degree wall; constraints become contradictory.
Meta-algorithmic magnification 5/5 broken 7/10 False degree bound; sparsity lands on the wrong side of the threshold.
Threshold degree-reduction normal form 5/5 broken 7/10 The enabling "margin-sparsity" lemma is false; the residual restates the target.
Easy-witness + win-win 5/5 broken 9/10 most broken Weight-taming lemma is equivalent to the open problem; degree wall kills it anyway.

The repair round — the decisive result

We took the least-broken attempt and tasked a fixer team with repairing the exact failure point. It could not be repaired. A genuine fix would require representing threshold functions with \(o(\sqrt n)\) approximate degree — that is, defeating an unconditional theorem. This is the single most valuable output of the round: the blocker is not a gap to be engineered around, it is a proven wall the technique conserves.

Bottom line

P vs NP remains open

Two rounds, nine adversarially adjudicated attempts, one repair round, and no survivor. The brute-force effort did not crack the problem; it produced a clean, externally checked map of why the leading techniques stop short.

The honestly live directions now point away from thresholds — for example the combinatorial Circuit-SAT channel decoupled from any threshold layer. None currently threatens the P vs NP boundary. Real progress still requires a genuinely new technique that evades all three classical barriers and does not conserve the threshold-degree wall.
