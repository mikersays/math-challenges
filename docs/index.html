Millennium Prize Problem

Can every quickly checked answer also be found quickly?

P vs NP asks whether efficient verification always implies efficient solution. The problem remains open, and decades of work have made the frontier unusually sharp: any proof must avoid several known families of failed techniques.

No proof of \(\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}\) or \(\mathrm{P}\ne\mathrm{NP}\) is known. Most researchers expect \(\mathrm{P}\ne\mathrm{NP}\), but that is evidence, not a theorem.

The question

Finding versus checking

P and NP are classes of decision problems: problems with yes/no answers. The distinction is whether a solution can be found quickly, or only checked quickly once someone hands you a certificate.

P Efficiently solvable

Problems solvable by a deterministic algorithm in polynomial time, such as \(n^2\), \(n^3\), or another fixed polynomial in the input size.

Example: deciding whether a path exists between two vertices in a graph.

NP Efficiently verifiable

Problems where a proposed solution can be checked in polynomial time. NP does not mean "not polynomial"; it means nondeterministic polynomial time.

Example: checking that a completed Sudoku grid satisfies every rule.

NP-complete Hardest in NP

Problems in NP to which every other NP problem can be translated efficiently. A polynomial-time algorithm for one NP-complete problem would put all of NP in P.

Examples: SAT, graph coloring, and the decision version of traveling salesman.

Because every quickly solvable problem is quickly checkable, \(\mathrm{P}\subseteq\mathrm{NP}\).

\[ \mathrm{P} \overset{?}{=} \mathrm{NP} \quad \text{or} \quad \mathrm{P}\subsetneq\mathrm{NP} \]

Why it matters

The answer would redraw practical and theoretical limits

Cryptography

If \(\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}\) came with practical algorithms, many public-key systems would be in danger. A nonconstructive or astronomically slow equality proof would be less immediately disruptive, which is why the algorithmic content matters.

Optimization

Scheduling, routing, verification, chip design, and search problems often contain NP-hard cores. A broad efficient solver would change what can be automated.

Mathematical limits

The problem is a test case for proving lower bounds: showing that some explicit task cannot be done efficiently by any general computation.

Why it is hard

Three barriers explain why familiar proof styles stall

These are not arguments that P vs NP is impossible to solve. They are theorems showing that broad categories of otherwise successful techniques cannot settle the question by themselves.

01

Relativization

Baker, Gill, and Solovay constructed oracle worlds where P and NP coincide and other oracle worlds where they separate. Pure diagonalization and simulation techniques relativize, so they cannot decide the real-world question alone.

02

Natural proofs

Razborov and Rudich showed that a large, constructive circuit lower-bound property strong enough for P/poly would also break standard pseudorandomness assumptions. Many known combinatorial lower bounds have exactly this natural form.

03

Algebrization

Aaronson and Wigderson showed that even arithmetization-style methods, which bypass relativization in some settings, still fail across algebraic oracle worlds tailored to P vs NP.
