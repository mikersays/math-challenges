Is P equal to NP?

The most famous open question in computer science asks whether every problem whose solution can be checked quickly can also be solved quickly. A definitive answer is worth one million dollars—and would reshape cryptography, optimization, and our understanding of what computers can do.

\[ \mathrm{P} \overset{?}{=} \mathrm{NP} \]

Status: OPEN — unresolved since it was formalized in 1971.

What is P vs NP?

P and NP are two classes of decision problems—questions with a yes/no answer. The conjecture concerns how the difficulty of finding an answer compares to the difficulty of verifying one.

P Polynomial time

The class of problems an ordinary (deterministic) computer can solve in time bounded by a polynomial in the input size—e.g. \(n^2\) or \(n^3\) steps. These are the problems we consider efficiently solvable.

Example: Is a number even? Is there a path between two cities?

NP Nondeterministic polynomial time

The class of problems for which a proposed solution—a certificate—can be verified in polynomial time. Finding the solution might be hard, but checking a candidate is easy.

Example: Given a Sudoku grid and a filled-in answer, confirming it is valid is quick.

NP-complete The hardest in NP

The "hardest" problems in NP: every problem in NP reduces to them in polynomial time. If any single NP-complete problem has a fast algorithm, then all of NP does—and \(\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}\).

Example: Boolean satisfiability (SAT), the travelling salesman decision problem, graph colouring.

The question, precisely

Every problem in P is in NP (if you can solve it quickly, you can certainly check it quickly), so \(\mathrm{P} \subseteq \mathrm{NP}\). The open question is whether that containment is strict:

\[ \text{Does } \mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}, \quad\text{or is}\quad \mathrm{P} \subsetneq \mathrm{NP}\,? \]

Equivalently, in the formal language-theoretic statement: does there exist a language that is verifiable in polynomial time but not decidable in polynomial time? Writing \(\mathrm{P} = \bigcup_{k} \mathrm{DTIME}(n^k)\) and \(\mathrm{NP} = \bigcup_{k} \mathrm{NTIME}(n^k)\), the conjecture is:

\[ \mathrm{P} \;\ne\; \mathrm{NP}. \]

Most researchers believe \(\mathrm{P} \ne \mathrm{NP}\), but this is a matter of evidence, not proof. \(\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}\) has not been ruled out.

Why it matters

This is not an abstract curiosity. The answer touches the foundations of security, the limits of automation, and the nature of creativity itself.

Cryptography

Modern encryption (RSA, elliptic-curve schemes, digital signatures) rests on problems believed to be hard—easy to verify, infeasible to break. If \(\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}\) with a practical algorithm, much of today's public-key cryptography would collapse overnight.

Optimization & discovery

Logistics, chip design, protein folding, scheduling, and theorem-proving are riddled with NP-hard problems. A fast general solver would turn many intractable searches—and arguably much of mathematical discovery—into routine computation.

The $1,000,000 prize

P vs NP is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems named by the Clay Mathematics Institute in 2000. A correct, peer-reviewed resolution carries a one-million-dollar award—a fitting bounty for a question this consequential.

Known barriers

Why is this so hard? Decades of work have produced three formal barrier theorems—meta-results proving that broad families of proof techniques cannot possibly settle the question. Any valid proof must evade all of them simultaneously.

  1. Relativization Baker–Gill–Solovay, 1975

    There exist oracles \(A\) and \(B\) with \(\mathrm{P}^{A} = \mathrm{NP}^{A}\) but \(\mathrm{P}^{B} \ne \mathrm{NP}^{B}\). Any technique that works the same way relative to any oracle—such as plain diagonalization and simulation—cannot decide the question, because the answer changes depending on the oracle.

  2. Natural proofs Razborov–Rudich, 1994

    Most combinatorial circuit lower-bound arguments are "natural": broad and constructive. Razborov and Rudich showed that a natural proof of strong lower bounds would break the very pseudorandom generators those bounds assume to exist—so, under standard cryptographic assumptions, natural proofs cannot separate P from NP.

  3. Algebrization Aaronson–Wigderson, 2008

    Arithmetization and interactive-proof techniques—which defeated relativization—were shown to "algebrize," extending to algebraic oracle settings. Algebrizing methods, too, are provably insufficient. It is not even known whether algebrization is the last barrier.

Together these results rule out essentially the entire classical toolkit—diagonalization, arithmetization, combinatorial circuit bounds, and brute-force search. We still have no super-polynomial lower bound for any explicit NP problem on a general basis (the record is roughly \(5n\) gates). Real progress demands a genuinely new, structure-exploiting technique that is at once non-relativizing, non-natural, and non-algebrizing.

Go deeper

We have mapped the frontier in detail—Williams' algorithmic method, meta-complexity and hardness magnification, Geometric Complexity Theory, algebraic lower bounds beyond the depth-4 chasm, and proof-complexity generators. None resolves the problem, but together they show precisely where the mathematics runs out.
